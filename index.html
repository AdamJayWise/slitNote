Spectral Resolution and Slit Width

...and why can't I set it to one micron?

Mechanical slits are a nearly universal feature of spectrometers. They restrict the light as it enters, forming a narrow aperture suitable for dispersing into a spectrum. A wider slit allows more light through, but the wider image it forms reduces the ability to resolve spectral features. A narrower slit creates fine spectral lines, but potentially at the cost of accepting less light from our source.

Hypothetically, if we had plentry of light and wanted to achieve the best possible spectral resolution, could we simply close the slit further and further, squeezing the light entering into a narrow and narrow line?

Sadly, no. First, even in a perfect world diffraction would eventually catch up with us, spreading out the image of our super-narrow slit. But before we hit that limit, there are two factors that limit the real-world resolution of the spectrometer.

Imaging Performance - PSF

No imaging system is perfectly sharp. The extent of this "blur" can be described as a point spread function (PSF) or line spread function (LSF). That is to say, the image of an infinitely-narrow slit will be broadened through imperfect imaging into a slightly-blurred line of finite width. At very small slit widths, the width of the image at the detector is dominated by the spectrometer's spread function. At larger slit widths, the small bit of blurring is inconsequential, and our image at the detector looks like the actual slit aperture. This is why the curve showing the relationship between slit width and resolution looks the way it does - it's linear when the slit width is large and the "fuzziness" at the edges is unimportant, but at small slit widths, the resolution hits the "floor" of the spectrometer imaging performance.

Pixel Size

The other factor that limits real-world spectrometer resolution is pixel size. Our image at the detector must be recorded, and with a digital camera this is done by recording the image across an array of pixels. The size of each pixel represents another hard limit for resolution. A super-narrow line projected onto a detector with big pixels will look exactly as wide as a single pixel - or sometimes two, if we're unlucky and hit the array of pixels right along a seam.
