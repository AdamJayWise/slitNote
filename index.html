What is the purpose of a spectrometer slit?

... and why can't I set it to one micron?

Mechanical slits, whether fixed, manually operated, or motorized, are an almost universal feature at the entrance port of a spectrometer. They let us shape the light entering the system, preparing a tall, thin source suitable for dispersing into a spectrum. For a light source with a large spatial extent - for example, the image of a bright light bulb focused with a magnifying glass onto the spectrometer's input - we can open an adjustable slit wide to let lots of light in, at the cost of decreased spectral resolution, or pinch it down into a thin vertical stripe, letting in a meagre amount of light, but shaped into an ideal vertical band perfect for spreading out in a well-resolved spectrum.

But can we close the slit down further and further until we achieve infinitely high spectral resolution? Sadly, no - eventually, the imaging performance of the spectrometer becomes the limiting factor. This limit can be described as a point spread function (PSF) or line spread function (LSF). That is to say, an infinitely-narrow slit will be broadened through imperfect imaging into a line of finite width. As the slit is opened the width of the slit combines with the spread function, at first making up only a small part of the total broadening, but at larger width dominating completely. This is why the curve showing the relationship between slit width and resolution looks the way it does - it's linear when the slit width is large and the "fuzziness" at the edges is inconsequentional, but at small slit widths, the resolution hits the "floor" of the spectrometer imaging performance.
